01The Mole: Chemistry's Counting Unit

Everything in this module depends on a single idea: chemists and biologists need a way to count particles (atoms, molecules, ions) that are far too small and numerous to count individually. The mole is that counting unit. One mole of anything contains 6.022 × 1023 particles. That number is Avogadro's number.

Key Point
The mole bridges the gap between the mass you can weigh on a balance and the number of particles actually present. This is the single most important conversion in all of chemistry and biology.

The molecular weight (or molar mass) of a substance tells you how many grams one mole of that substance weighs. For example, the molecular weight of NaCl is about 58.44 g/mol, meaning one mole of NaCl weighs 58.44 grams and contains 6.022 × 1023 formula units of NaCl.

Moles from Mass
moles = mass (g) / molecular weight (g/mol)
Worked Example

You weigh out 11.69 g of NaCl. How many moles is that?

11.69 g / 58.44 g/mol = 0.200 mol

This means you have 0.200 × 6.022 × 1023 = 1.2 × 1023 formula units of NaCl.

Common Mistake
Students often confuse molecular weight with the mass of a sample. Molecular weight is a fixed property of a substance (grams per mole). The mass of your sample depends on how much you weighed out. They are related by the formula above, but they are not the same thing.

02Molarity

Molarity is the most common way to express the concentration of a solution in both chemistry and biology. It tells you how many moles of solute are dissolved in one liter of solution. The unit is moles per liter, abbreviated M.

Molarity
M = moles of solute / liters of solution
Units: mol/L, which is the same as saying "molar" or "M"

A 1 M solution of glucose means there is 1 mole of glucose dissolved in enough water to make exactly 1 liter of total solution. Notice it says "liters of solution," not "liters of water." The solute takes up space too, so you dissolve the solute first, then add water to reach your final volume.

Worked Example

You need 500 mL of a 0.15 M NaCl solution. How many grams of NaCl do you weigh out?

Step 1: Find moles needed. 0.15 mol/L × 0.500 L = 0.075 mol

Step 2: Convert moles to grams. 0.075 mol × 58.44 g/mol = 4.38 g

Dissolve 4.38 g NaCl in water, then bring the total volume to 500 mL.

Key Point
Molarity counts the number of moles of the substance you dissolved (the formula you weighed out), regardless of what happens to it after it dissolves. If you dissolve 1 mole of NaCl, the molarity of NaCl is 1 M, even though it breaks apart into Na+ and Cl− in water. This distinction becomes critical when we get to osmolarity.

Cross-Disciplinary Bridge
Molarity shows up in general chemistry (stoichiometry of reactions in solution), biology (cell culture media, osmotic calculations), biochemistry (enzyme kinetics, buffer preparation), and clinical medicine (IV fluid concentrations, drug dosing). The concept is identical everywhere. Only the context changes.

03Dilutions

Dilution means adding more solvent to a solution to decrease its concentration. The essential idea is that the number of moles of solute does not change when you dilute. You are only adding water (or other solvent), not adding or removing solute.

Dilution Equation
M1V1 = M2V2
M1 = initial concentration, V1 = volume you take from the stock, M2 = final concentration, V2 = final total volume

This works because M × V gives you moles: (mol/L) × L = mol. Since the moles stay the same before and after dilution, M1V1 must equal M2V2.

Worked Example

You have a 5 M stock solution of glucose. You need 200 mL of a 0.25 M solution. How much stock do you use?

M1V1 = M2V2

(5 M)(V1) = (0.25 M)(0.200 L)

V1 = 0.050 L / 5 = 0.010 L = 10 mL

Take 10 mL of stock, add water to a total volume of 200 mL.

Common Mistake
Students sometimes think V2 is the amount of water you add. It is not. V2 is the total final volume. If you need 200 mL total and you took 10 mL of stock, you add 190 mL of water, not 200 mL.

04Dissociation & Electrolytes

This section is where chemistry and biology start to overlap in a way that confuses students, because the two disciplines often teach the same concept under different names and in different chapters. The core idea: some substances break apart into ions when they dissolve in water, and some do not.

Key Point
Molarity counts formula units. Osmolarity counts particles. The difference between them is dissociation.

When NaCl dissolves in water, every formula unit separates into one Na+ ion and one Cl− ion. So 1 mole of NaCl produces 2 moles of particles in solution. Glucose (C6H12O6), on the other hand, does not dissociate. One mole of glucose produces 1 mole of particles.

The number of particles a substance produces when it dissolves is called the van 't Hoff factor (i). For substances that dissociate completely, i equals the total number of ions produced. For nonelectrolytes, i = 1.

van 't Hoff Factor
i = total moles of particles in solution / moles of solute dissolved
For ideal strong electrolytes: i = number of ions per formula unit
Cross-Disciplinary Bridge
In general chemistry, you learn about strong and weak electrolytes when studying solution chemistry. In biology, the same concept reappears as "osmotically active particles" when you study cell membranes and osmosis. In physiology and nursing, it shows up again when you calculate IV fluid osmolarity. These are all the same idea: how many independent particles are floating around in solution.

05Osmolarity & Tonicity

Osmolarity is the total concentration of all dissolved particles in a solution, measured in osmoles per liter (Osm/L) or more commonly milliosmoles per liter (mOsm/L). This is the number that actually matters for osmosis, because water movement depends on the total number of dissolved particles, not on what kind of particles they are.

Osmolarity
Osmolarity = Molarity × i
where i = van 't Hoff factor (number of particles per formula unit)
Worked Example

What is the osmolarity of 0.9% NaCl (normal saline)?

Step 1: Convert % to molarity. 0.9% w/v = 0.9 g per 100 mL = 9 g/L. 9 g/L / 58.44 g/mol = 0.154 M

Step 2: NaCl dissociates into 2 particles (i = 2). 0.154 M × 2 = 0.308 Osm/L = 308 mOsm/L

This is very close to the osmolarity of human blood plasma (275–295 mOsm/L), which is why normal saline is used as an isotonic IV fluid.

Osmolarity vs. Osmolality

Osmolarity is osmoles per liter of solution. Osmolality is osmoles per kilogram of solvent. In dilute aqueous solutions (which covers nearly all biological and clinical situations), the two values are essentially the same because the density of dilute solutions is close to 1 g/mL. Clinical labs usually report osmolality (measured by freezing point depression), but the terms are often used interchangeably in practice.

Tonicity vs. Osmolarity

Tonicity describes the effect a solution has on cell volume. It depends only on the concentration of non-penetrating solutes, meaning solutes that cannot cross the cell membrane. Osmolarity counts all solutes, penetrating and non-penetrating.

Common Mistake
A solution can be hyperosmolar but isotonic. For example, a solution of urea may have a high osmolarity, but because urea freely crosses cell membranes, it does not create an osmotic gradient, and cells placed in it will not shrink. Tonicity depends on whether the solute can cross the membrane, not just on how much of it there is.

06Osmosis

Osmosis is the net movement of water across a selectively permeable membrane from a region of lower solute concentration (higher water concentration) to a region of higher solute concentration (lower water concentration). Water moves toward the solute. Think of it this way: water goes where the "stuff" is.

Key Point
Water moves toward the side with more dissolved particles. This is the single sentence that explains osmosis. If you can identify which side has more particles, you know which direction water will flow.

Osmosis is passive. It requires no energy input and occurs spontaneously whenever there is a concentration gradient across a semipermeable membrane. The membrane must be permeable to water but impermeable (or less permeable) to the solute for osmosis to occur.

Osmotic Pressure

Osmotic pressure (π) is the pressure that would need to be applied to the high-solute side to prevent water from crossing. It is a measure of the "pulling power" of dissolved particles for water.

Osmotic Pressure (van 't Hoff Equation)
π = iMRT
i = van 't Hoff factor, M = molarity, R = 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K, T = temperature in Kelvin
Worked Example

What is the osmotic pressure of a 0.30 M glucose solution at 37°C (body temperature)?

Glucose does not dissociate, so i = 1. T = 37 + 273 = 310 K.

π = (1)(0.30)(0.0821)(310) = 7.64 atm

That is a surprisingly large pressure, about 7.6 times atmospheric pressure, generated by a relatively dilute sugar solution. This illustrates how powerful osmotic forces can be in biological systems.

Cross-Disciplinary Bridge
The van 't Hoff equation (π = iMRT) has the same form as the ideal gas law (PV = nRT). This is not a coincidence. Dissolved particles exert osmotic pressure the way gas particles exert gas pressure, and the mathematical relationship is the same. If you know the ideal gas law from chemistry, you already know the framework for osmotic pressure.

07Cells in Solutions

When you place a cell in a solution, water will move by osmosis depending on the relative concentration of non-penetrating solutes inside and outside the cell. The terminology is defined from the perspective of the solution the cell is placed in.

Solution TypeSolute ConcentrationWater MovementAnimal CellPlant Cell
HypotonicLower outside than insideWater enters cellSwells, may lyse (burst)Becomes turgid (firm), ideal state
IsotonicEqual inside and outsideNo net movementNormal shapeFlaccid (limp)
HypertonicHigher outside than insideWater leaves cellCrenation (shrivels)Plasmolysis (membrane pulls from wall)
Key Point
"Hypo" means below (less solute outside), "hyper" means above (more solute outside), "iso" means equal. The prefixes refer to the solute concentration of the external solution relative to the cell interior.
Common Mistake
Students often mix up the direction: in a hypotonic solution, water goes IN (cell swells), not out. Remember: water follows the solute. In a hypotonic solution, there is more solute INSIDE the cell, so water flows inward. The prefix describes the solution, not the cell.
Worked Example

A red blood cell (osmolarity about 300 mOsm/L) is placed in 0.45% NaCl. What happens?

0.45% NaCl = 4.5 g/L. Molarity = 4.5/58.44 = 0.077 M. Osmolarity = 0.077 × 2 = 154 mOsm/L.

154 mOsm/L is lower than the cell's 300 mOsm/L, so the solution is hypotonic. Water enters the cell. The red blood cell swells and may burst (hemolysis).

Cross-Disciplinary Bridge
Plants actually need a slightly hypotonic environment. The influx of water creates turgor pressure that pushes the plasma membrane against the rigid cell wall, making the plant firm and upright. When plants wilt, it means they've lost turgor pressure because there isn't enough water entering the cells. This connects cell biology (membrane transport), botany (plant structure), and chemistry (concentration gradients) in a single phenomenon.

08Colligative Properties

Colligative properties are properties of a solution that depend on the number of dissolved particles, not on what those particles are. There are four colligative properties: boiling point elevation, freezing point depression, vapor pressure lowering, and osmotic pressure. These are taught in general chemistry, but they connect directly to the biology of osmosis because they all depend on particle count.

Boiling Point Elevation
ΔTb = i × Kb × m
Kb for water = 0.512 °C/m, m = molality (mol solute/kg solvent)
Freezing Point Depression
ΔTf = i × Kf × m
Kf for water = 1.86 °C/m

Notice that all colligative property formulas include i, the van 't Hoff factor. This is the same i that appears in osmolarity calculations and the osmotic pressure equation. The thread running through everything in this module is: particles matter, and how many of them you have determines what happens.

Cross-Disciplinary Bridge
Clinical labs measure blood osmolality by freezing point depression. The more particles dissolved in blood, the lower the freezing point. This connects a general chemistry concept (colligative properties) directly to a clinical diagnostic technique used in hospitals every day. Salting roads in winter works by the same principle: dissolved salt lowers the freezing point of water.
Common Mistake
Colligative property formulas use molality (m, moles per kg solvent), not molarity (M, moles per liter solution). In dilute aqueous solutions the numbers are nearly the same, but the distinction matters for precise calculations and exam questions.

09Clinical & Lab Connections

The concepts in this module are not abstract. They are used daily in clinical medicine, research labs, and anywhere solutions are prepared.

Normal Saline (0.9% NaCl)

This is the most commonly used IV fluid. Its osmolarity of approximately 308 mOsm/L is close enough to blood plasma (275–295 mOsm/L) that it functions as an isotonic solution. Red blood cells placed in normal saline neither swell nor shrink.

D5W (5% Dextrose in Water)

Initially isotonic in the bag (because glucose is present), but once infused, the glucose is rapidly metabolized, leaving behind free water. The effective result is a hypotonic infusion that distributes water into cells.

Serial Dilutions

In microbiology and immunology, serial dilutions are used to determine antibody titers, bacterial counts, and drug concentrations. Each step is a dilution (M1V1 = M2V2) applied repeatedly. A 1:10 serial dilution starting from 1 M goes to 0.1 M, then 0.01 M, then 0.001 M, and so on.

Osmolar Gap

In emergency medicine, the osmolar gap (measured osmolality minus calculated osmolality) helps detect poisoning by methanol, ethylene glycol, or other osmotically active substances. The chemistry concept of osmolarity is used as a diagnostic tool.

Key Point
Normal human blood plasma osmolarity: 275–295 mOsm/L. Normal saline: about 308 mOsm/L. These two numbers are worth memorizing because they anchor everything about IV fluid therapy and osmotic calculations in a clinical context.
